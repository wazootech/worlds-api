---
title: "Manifesto"
description: "The next medium for human knowledge"
---

The story of human progress is the story of how information is stored, shared,
and processed. Every era follows the same pattern: a new medium is born—from
ochre paintings on stone to spreadsheets on silicon—and civilization surges
forward.

For the vast majority of the human era, the brain was our only hard drive. Early
societies used [myth and song](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oral_tradition) as
mnemonic devices to encode critical data across generations.

The Agricultural Revolution forced humanity to manage surpluses and trade,
catalyzing the invention of
[**Cuneiform**](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuneiform). Writing did not begin
as a way to record history; it began as a spreadsheet. Later, the Phoenicians
introduced the
[**phonetic alphabet**](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phoenician_alphabet),
democratizing literacy across the Mediterranean. Millennia later, the
[**printing press**](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Printing_press) collapsed the
cost of replication, breaking the state monopoly on information and fueling the
[Renaissance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renaissance) and the
[Scientific Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_Revolution).

By the 20th century, we reached the physical limits of paper. In 1945, the
concept of the [**Memex**](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Memex) emerged,
recognizing that the human mind operates by _association_, not rigid
hierarchical folders. It proposed that information systems must allow users to
map associative trails between arbitrary items—the first conceptual model for a
knowledge graph.

This was formalized in 1968 through the demonstration of
[hypertext](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypertext) and
[transclusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transclusion), and later realized
in pioneering systems at
[**Xerox PARC**](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PARC_(company)>) and Apple's
[**HyperCard**](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HyperCard). These systems proved
the profound power of natively associative, interconnected knowledge. However,
as computing rapidly commercialized for the masses in the 1980s, the industry
standardized on the **desktop metaphor**. By organizing data into isolated
constraints, such as files inside folders, it trapped data inside proprietary
application silos, severing those associative links entirely.

As the Internet scaled, the
[W3C](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Wide_Web_Consortium) championed the
[**Resource Description Framework (RDF)**](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resource_Description_Framework),
an interoperable standard for representing linked data.

Today, we are crossing a threshold. With the rise of autonomous agents, the
externalized hard drive has learned to think. The medium itself can read,
synthesize, pattern-match, and generate knowledge.

**Worlds** realizes the original vision of the Semantic Web converged with the
bleeding edge of AI. It provides the stateful substrate required to unhobble
autonomous agents by powering Neuro-Symbolic AI—fusing the unstructured
flexibility of
[Large Language Models](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_language_model) with
the verifiable structure of the
[Semantic Web](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semantic_Web).

Agents can now autonomously query, reason over, and proactively update a
universe of world knowledge.

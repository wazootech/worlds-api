## 1945–1968: The visionaries

Human coordination begins with the externalization of thought. From the Andean
**Khipu** to Douglas Engelbart’s **Mother of All Demos**, researchers expand
human capability through symbolic manipulation.

Engelbart’s 1962 paper, _Augmenting Human Intellect_, argued that computers
should evolve the way we think rather than just automate tasks. He demonstrated
**transclusion**—the ability to view a live instance of data across multiple
contexts without duplication.

## 1980s: The era of structural friction (The desktop detour)

In systems design, intentional friction forces adaptation. The industry’s
adoption of the **Desktop Metaphor** in the 1980s prioritized ease of use at the
cost of structural integrity. By organizing data into rigid "folders" and
"apps," the system neutralized the cognitive load of computing but severed the
associative links envisioned by Bush and Engelbart.

This detour created **application silos**. Data became a "hostage" to specific
interfaces. This friction forces us today to rediscover the "Item Store"
methodology.

## The future: Beyond the app

The Worlds ecosystem removes the app icon. It treats data as a **universal item
store** where:

- **AI agents** possess persistent, neuro-symbolic memory.
- **Transclusion** replaces copy-pasting.
- **Users** mutate and fork graphs in real-time.

Worlds provides the memory layer for the next generation of intelligent systems,
enabling a post-app world.

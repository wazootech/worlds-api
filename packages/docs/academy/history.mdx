title: "History of the Worlds ecosystem"
description:
  "Tracing the evolution of communication and computing from early language to
  the global item store."
---

The **Worlds ecosystem** builds upon nearly a century of foundational computing
research. Universal data portability, item graphs, and transclusion drive this
framework.

## Key takeaways

- Human coordination relies on the externalization of thought through symbols
  and alphabets.
- Early computing pioneers envisioned associative, interlinked information
  systems.
- Worlds restores the vision of a linked ecosystem by treating data as a
  universal item store.

## Early history of communication

Human coordination begins with the externalization of thought. Early humans drew
images on cave walls to symbolize physical objects. This symbolic representation
allowed individuals to share survival tactics through shared meaning.

Civilization later moved from pictorial symbols to the **Phoenician alphabet**.
This invention froze language in physical space. The **Gutenberg press** later
mechanized this recording process. This mechanization enabled ideas to travel as
fast as physical transit.

## Ancient data interfaces: The khipu

Humans pioneered sophisticated, non-alphabetic ways to interface with data. The
[Khipu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quipu) is a system of knotted strings used
to record information. These Andean devices utilized knot types, colors, and
textures to store data. These multidimensional item graphs recorded both numeric
records and narrative histories. The loss of specialized knowledge during the
Spanish conquest destroyed the ability to read these records.

## 1945: The memex

Vannevar Bush proposed the **memex** in 1945. His essay _"As We May Think"_
envisioned a mechanical desk that interlinked separate texts. This design
mirrored the associative nature of the human mind.

## 1960s: Hypertext and augmenting intellect

Douglas Engelbart published _"Augmenting Human Intellect"_ in 1962. He argued
that computers should expand human capability. At the 1968 "Mother of All
Demos," he demonstrated **hypertext** and **transclusion**. Transclusion enables
a live view of one document inside another document.

## 1980s: The desktop detour

Alan Kay viewed the personal computer as a medium that changes thought patterns.
The industry adopted the **desktop metaphor** to simplify computers for the
general public. This metaphor organized digital data into folders and apps. This
structure severed the natural links between related pieces of information.

## 1990s: The global graph and compound documents

Tim Berners-Lee turned public information into a densely linked graph with the
World Wide Web. Systems like **HyperCard** and **OpenDoc** explored deep
integration. These platforms treated software as a collection of reusable
components.

## 2000s to present: The Worlds ecosystem

Personal wikis and **notes graphs** like Obsidian maintained the vision of a
linked ecosystem. The **Worlds ecosystem** now removes the app icon. It treats
data as a universal item store rather than isolated files. This approach brings
universal linking and malleability to the core of digital environments.
